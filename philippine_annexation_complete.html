The League argued that imperialism contradicted founding principles, specifically the Declaration of Independence's assertion of "consent of the governed." McKinley's framing of US rule as temporary and humanitarian â€” promising to "educate," "uplift," and prepare Filipinos for self-government. Federal law banning Chinese immigration and barring Chinese residents from naturalization. It was the first US law to exclude an entire ethnic group, and was later extended by the Geary Act (1892). The United States entered the 1890s in crisis. The Panic of 1893 triggered one of the nation's most severe depressions, lasting four years. Banks collapsed by the thousands. Unemployment soared. Spain sued for peace.

The Treaty of Paris, signed December 10, 1898, transferred territory from Spain to the United States: Puerto Rico, Guam, and â€” most significantly â€” the Philippines. The price was $20 million. Crucially, no Filipinos were present to consent to or object to this transfer. In the same year, the United States also annexed HawaiÊ»i via the Newlands Resolution (rather than through a treaty that would have required a two-thirds Senate vote). Part II: The War

Before the Americans: The Philippine Revolution

The Philippines were not a blank slate awaiting American tutelage. In August 1896, Filipinos launched a revolution against Spanish rule. Emilio Aguinaldo, a revolutionary leader, led the movement. Spain exiled him to Hong Kong in 1897. In May 1898 â€” during the Spanish-American War â€” Dewey's victory at Manila Bay prompted the U.S. Navy to bring Aguinaldo back from Hong Kong to help rally anti-Spanish forces. By late 1898, Filipinos controlled most of the archipelago.

On August 13, 1898, U.S. forces took Manila in what historians often describe as a prearranged U.S.â€“Spanish surrender â€” a "mock battle" orchestrated so Spain could capitulate to the Americans rather than to Filipino forces. Aguinaldo's army, which had fought and died against Spain, was shut out of the capital. At the Treaty of Paris in December, Filipinos were not present and had no voice in the transfer of sovereignty. On December 21, McKinley issued his "Benevolent Assimilation" proclamation, promising to "educate," "uplift," and prepare Filipinos for self-government â€” but under American control, with no timeline specified. Many Filipino leaders experienced this sequence as a betrayal: they had allied with the United States against a common enemy, only to discover that American sovereignty would replace Spanish sovereignty.

On February 4, 1899, shooting broke out in Manila. Both sides blamed the other for initiating. On January 20, 1899, even as war was breaking out, McKinley established the Schurman Commission (headed by Cornell University president Jacob Schurman) to gather information about Filipino populations, their legislative needs, and how the U.S. might maintain order and administer the islands. This reveals an important pattern: even as the military was fighting, the political and bureaucratic machinery of occupation was already being designed. The U.S. was planning colonial administration before the outcome of the war was certain.

The Conventional Phase (Februaryâ€“November 1899)

The Philippine Republic fielded a conventional army against superior American firepower. U.S. forces, better armed and trained, won every major engagement. By November 1899, the conventional army was shattered. Aguinaldo made a strategic decision: dissolve the regular army and shift to guerrilla warfare.

The Guerrilla Phase (November 1899â€“1902)

From November 1899 onward, the war transformed into small-unit ambushes, nighttime raids, and intelligence networks embedded in civilian populations. The Filipino strategy aimed to make war so costly in American lives and resources that American public opinion would turn against the occupation. The U.S. responded with escalating counterinsurgency tactics drawn directly from the Indian Wars: scorched-earth campaigns, forced reconcentration of civilian populations, collective punishment of villages suspected of harboring fighters, reprisals, and the systematic destruction of food supplies. The line between combatant and civilian was deliberately blurred as a strategic tool. Villages were burned. The water cure â€” a torture technique forcing water down a person's throat â€” was deployed. American soldiers wrote home about the brutality, but these accounts were often censored or suppressed.

The End

Three developments broke Filipino resistance: In March 1901, American forces captured Aguinaldo â€” a psychological blow that suggested the war was lost. Reconcentration policies isolated fighters from civilian support networks, though at enormous humanitarian cost. And the transition to civilian governance under William Howard Taft signaled that the U.S. was settling in for permanent rule, making continued military resistance seem futile. On July 4, 1902, President Roosevelt issued a full amnesty proclamation and declared the war officially over. Armed resistance, particularly in Mindanao, continued for over a decade.

Paradoxically, the very war that aimed to annihilate Filipino independence aspirations forged a unified Filipino national identity. Before 1898, the Philippines were a collection of kingdoms, sultanates, and ethnic regions with distinct languages and traditions â€” not a unified nation. The shared experience of resistance, the figures who died fighting, and the guerrilla campaign itself created the emotional and institutional basis for Filipino nationalism. The United States did not "civilize" Filipinos into being; it gave them a reason to imagine themselves as one nation.

Part III: Race, the Indian Wars, and the Logic of Colonial Violence

The Philippine-American War did not happen in isolation from American history. It was a direct, institutional, tactical, and ideological continuation of the Indian Wars. As historian Paul Kramer argues, race was not incidental to the war â€” it was structural. The war did not just reflect existing racism; it produced new racial categories. American soldiers and officers "tribalized" Filipinos, describing them as "savages" needing pacification, not as potential fellow citizens.

The Pattern

In North America, the pattern was clear: the United States purchased or conquered territory, then fought Native Americans to colonize and "civilize" it. In the Philippines, the U.S. purchased rights from Spain, then fought Filipinos to pacify and govern them. In both cases, resistance was racialized as savagery requiring pacification rather than as legitimate political opposition.

Personnel: An Army Shaped by the Indian Wars

A significant share of senior U.S. commanders sent to the Philippines had built their careers in the Indian Wars, and they carried that repertoire â€” tactically and rhetorically â€” into the Pacific (Aune). This was not accidental â€” it was deliberate policy. The Army believed that experience fighting Native Americans was the best credential for fighting Filipinos. General Elwell S. Otis, commander of U.S. forces in the Philippines early in the war, had spent decades in frontier campaigns. General Samuel B. M. Young, who had served in the campaigns against Geronimo, was deployed as a senior counterinsurgency commander.

The Credential

"General Lawton, whose life has been spent in fighting Indians, is the best equipped man in the army for this peculiar service." This Army officer, describing General Henry W. Lawton's qualifications for fighting Filipinos, shows how the Indian Wars experience was explicitly valued as preparation for empire.

Tactics: Frontier Methods Transferred

The tactical transfer was direct. Young's Scouts â€” a 25-man elite unit from the 1877 Nez Perce War â€” conducted "rice burning expeditions" in the Philippines, mirroring the buffalo destruction campaigns of the Indian Wars. The U.S. forced Filipino civilians into reconcentration zones (mirroring Spain's similar tactic in Cuba, which Americans had condemned as barbaric). American officers burned barrios to deny supplies to guerrillas. The water cure was deployed. General Jacob Smith issued an extermination order to kill anyone over age ten in certain zones.

"Every barrio... will be burned, if necessary to effect a complete destruction of the enemy." â€” General J. Franklin Bell, 1901. "I want no prisoners. I wish you to kill and burn." â€” General Jacob Smith, 1901.

Ideology: Race-Making in Real Time

Soldiers called Filipinos "Indians" and spoke of combat as "hunting" â€” directly mapping Indian Wars language onto a new enemy. As guerrilla resistance intensified, American military escalation and racialization fed each other in a spiral. Soldiers portrayed Filipinos as "tribal" peoples incapable of understanding civilization, law, or self-governance. The worse the fighting, the more "savage" and "childlike" the enemy was described as being. Contemporary soldier diaries reveal this pattern: the more violence the U.S. employed, the more the enemy was described as deserving it. Kramer argues that the war generated new racial logic in real time â€” the act of war itself produced the "knowledge" that Filipinos were racially inferior.

Private Charles S. Kinne kept a diary of his service in the Philippines. Traveling west by train before shipping out, Kinne passed through the American West â€” lands that had been "pacified" during the Indian Wars. Native Americans were confined to reservations. The landscape had been colonized. For Kinne and soldiers like him, the Philippines was simply the next frontier â€” the logical extension of a project that had already fundamentally reshaped North America.

Cultural Celebration

Children's books by Edward Stratemeyer and other authors celebrated General Lawton as a hero, first in the Indian Wars and then in the Philippines, making both conflicts feel like chapters in a single, heroic American narrative.

Part IV: Building a Colonial State

The Schurman Commission Report

In January 1900, the Schurman Commission issued its findings. It had investigated Filipino political aspirations, legal traditions, and governance capacity. The Commission found that Filipinos desired religious freedom, human rights, and home rule â€” in other words, self-determination. But the Commission concluded that Filipinos were "incapable of self-governance." Empire became pedagogy: the United States would not simply rule; it would teach Filipinos how to govern themselves, on a timeline determined entirely by Americans.

Taft and the Second Commission

William Howard Taft, a federal judge appointed as head of the Second Philippine Commission, summarized his racial framework bluntly: Filipinos were "ignorant, superstitious, and credulous." He predicted they would need "fifty or a hundred years" to understand the principles of Anglo-Saxon liberty and self-governance. Taft designed a comprehensive colonial governance system: civilian administration replacing military rule, public works projects (roads, schools, courts), and the promise of eventual "limited self-governance" â€” but always under ultimate American sovereignty. In Taft's view, delivering tangible benefits would convince Filipinos that American rule was benevolent, making resistance seem unreasonable.

Commission Government

On September 1, 1900, the Philippine Commission assumed legislative powers. The Commission consisted of Americans appointed by the President. There was no Filipino input into governance. Taft believed that if Americans made the trains run on time and built schools, Filipinos would accept rule without consent.

The Spooner Amendment and Civil Government

In March 1901, Congress passed the Spooner Amendment, which codified Taft's vision into law. On July 4, 1901, Taft became Civil Governor of the Philippines. The Philippine Organic Act of 1902 organized the archipelago as an "unorganized territory" (not granted a path to statehood), created an elected Philippine Assembly with limited powers, and allowed the Philippines to send two Resident Commissioners to observe Congress (but not vote). The legal architecture was now in place: empire, bureaucratized and normalized.

The Collaborative Empire

The American occupation succeeded â€” to the extent it did â€” because it found local collaborators. The ilustrados â€” wealthy, educated Filipino elites â€” often cooperated with the Americans for economic and political reasons. Benito Legarda, who had initially opposed annexation, shifted to collaboration. The Partido Federal formed in 1900â€“1901 to support American rule. This was not passive acceptance; it was rational calculation by Filipino leaders who could not defeat American military power but believed they might shape its exercise. They bargained for seats on commissions, government positions, and influence over policy. Some genuinely believed American governance would modernize the islands; others simply accepted the new reality.

Institution-Building as Empire

Here is the paradox: empire was built not merely through military force but through institution-building. The Commission, the civil government, the school system, the courts, the bureaucracy â€” these structures made occupation permanent and normalized it. By 1902, the Philippines was organized as a colonial state integrated into American law, administration, and bureaucracy. Removing the Americans would require dismantling these institutions, a far more complex task than military resistance alone.

Part V: Race and the Politics of Empire

Race as Justification

Expansionists made racial arguments for annexation. Albert J. Beveridge called expansion "elemental" and "racial," a "divine mission." He described Filipinos as "children utterly incapable of self-government." William Howard Taft called them "little brown brothers" â€” a paternalism that acknowledged their humanity while denying their capacity. Both sides agreed: race explained why Filipinos needed American rule.

Race as Opposition

Anti-imperialists also used race as their central argument â€” but to opposite effect. Champ Clark of Missouri opposed Hawaiian annexation because of the islands' Asian and Pacific Islander populations. He warned that annexing Hawaii would introduce "polyglot" (mixed-race) populations into the American body politic, threatening racial homogeneity. Race made empire dangerous because it would force the United States to govern nonwhite peoples and (potentially) make them citizens.

The Paradox of Racial Argument

Both sides of the debate used identical racialized logic to reach opposite conclusions. Expansionists: "Filipinos are racially inferior; therefore they need our civilizing mission." Anti-imperialists: "Filipinos are racially inferior; therefore we cannot safely make them citizens or govern them without becoming corrupted ourselves." The debate was not about whether race mattered â€” the entire political spectrum accepted racial hierarchy as a fundamental fact. The argument was about what to do with these "inferior races." Both sides assumed racial ordering; they disagreed only on the policy response.

The Hawaiian Precedent

Hawaii annexed in 1898 had substantial Japanese and Chinese populations. If the U.S. annexed Hawaii (and later the Philippines), would Asian residents and their descendants become American citizens? This raised an unresolvable contradiction: the United States was simultaneously maintaining a racial ban on Asian immigration and naturalization (the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882) while acquiring territory with Asian populations. How could the nation exclude Asians from citizenship in California while governing Asian peoples in Hawaii and the Philippines?

Social Darwinism as Framework

Underneath both sides of the debate lay a pseudo-scientific consensus: Social Darwinism. The idea was that races competed for survival and dominance, that some were more "evolved" than others, and that this competition was natural and inevitable. Educated Americans across the political spectrum â€” expansionists, anti-imperialists, labor leaders, reformers â€” accepted this racial premise. The argument was never whether races were unequal; it was whether inequality justified annexation or precluded it.

The Partisan Roots of Anti-Imperialism

The post-Reconstruction partisan realignment dramatically changed the nature of anti-imperialist activism in the United States. In the years before the Civil War, suspicion of Slave Power and anti-imperialism complemented each other, with a more reformist vision at home accompanying a vision for a less militaristic foreign policy. During the Gilded Age, however, anti-imperialism became more associated with the right, especially Southern Democrats. Their interest in the cause was almost entirely cynical: a fear of creating a precedent for a more powerful national government that could, at least in theory, be applied against the racial caste system that they so strongly defended. The result was a dizzying array of imperialist ventures launched by Republican administrations eager to expand U.S. economic might that were reversed during the Gilded Age periods when Democrats dominated Congress or the White House.21

The clearest example of the pattern involved the Frelinghuysen-Zavala Treaty, in which the United States sought a protectorate over Nicaragua to facilitate construction of a trans-Isthmian canal. But Republicans narrowly lost the 1884 presidential election to Grover Cleveland, and the new president promptly withdrew the treaty from consideration. His secretary of state, former Delaware senator Thomas Bayard, reflected post-Civil War sentiments on the need for a limited government, including in foreign affairs, and retained a cautious approach diplomatically for the next four years.22

The best-known Gilded Age debate about imperialism revolved around the U.S. relationship with the Kingdom of HawaiÊ»i. The strategically located islands had generated a strategic, economic, and cultural rivalry between the United States, Britain, and eventually Japan, which intensified as the 19th century proceeded. In 1893, with the tacit support of Republicans in Washington, U.S. business interests launched a coup against Queen Liliuokalani and proclaimed a HawaiÊ»ian "republic." The new entity quickly petitioned the U.S. government for formal annexation. By the time of the coup, the 1892 election had replaced Benjamin Harrison with Cleveland â€” who, as he had earlier with regards to Nicaragua, refused to endorse an imperialist venture. But Cleveland's anti-imperialism was of a limited variety. His administration declined the queen's request to help restore her to power, a venture in which congressional Democrats likewise displayed little interest. When Cleveland's Democrats lost power following the 1896 elections, the new administration of William McKinley revived the annexation idea, and Hawaiian independence ended.

An Imperial Moment: Treaty Ratification and Its Politics

The limits of this more conservative version of anti-imperialism became clear in the aftermath of the Spanish-American War. McKinley's administration accompanied a conflict with Spain over Cuba with an ambitious plan to seize Spanish colonies in Puerto Rico and the Philippines â€” the latter despite the existence of an anti-colonial revolt that envisioned an independent republic under Emilio Aguinaldo. The war ended with an 1899 treaty that transferred Spanish sovereignty in the Philippines (and Puerto Rico) to the United States. Though Republicans possessed a Senate majority, McKinley needed some Democratic support to get the requisite two-thirds for approval.

As had occurred in the 1850s, the existence of aggressive political and ideological defenses of imperialism stimulated a powerful anti-imperialist dissent. But by this point, the ideological focus of anti-imperialism had shifted considerably. Sumner's successor in the Senate, George Hoar, charged that imperialists believed "we have outgrown the principles and the interpretation which were sufficient for our thirteen states and our 3 million people in the time of their weakness," thereby embracing an imperialist doctrine that "would make of every war between civilized and powerful nations a war of extermination or a war of dishonor." Hoar was blunt: "You have no right at the cannon's mouth to impose on an unwilling people your Declaration of Independence and your Constitution and your notions of freedom and notions of what is good."23

In the end, however, Hoar was one of only two Republican senators to vote against the treaty. Its fate â€” reflecting the new politics of anti-imperialism â€” came down to Southern Democrats. Democrats who opposed acquisition of the Philippines borrowed from John Calhoun's approach of stressing racism and limited government to make their case. But this anti-imperialism was of a limited variety â€” most Southern Democrats did not oppose imperialism per se. Instead, they feared how an imperialistic foreign policy could threaten Jim Crow, both ideologically and constitutionally.24 This viewpoint provided an opening for the McKinley administration: the president ultimately got the treaty approved through a deal with Louisiana Senator Samuel McEnery, in which McEnery agreed to vote for the treaty in exchange for McKinley granting him control over federal patronage in the state.25

The Anti-Imperialist League

The battle over the Philippines stimulated the largest grassroots foreign policy organization in American history until that time. The Anti-Imperialist League, which claimed a membership of 250,000, deemed McKinley's efforts in the Philippines a "criminal aggression" that would "extinguish the spirit of 1776 in those islands." In addition to its membership base, the League attracted high-profile support, including philanthropist Andrew Carnegie and satirist Mark Twain. "I am an anti-imperialist," Twain proclaimed. "I am opposed to having the eagle put its talons on any other land." He worried that "we have gone there to conquer, not to redeem." But the League was, in many ways, of a different era; its mostly Northeastern base harkened back to the ties between abolitionism and anti-imperialism. Its internal divisions were intensified by debates over endorsing Democrat William Jennings Bryan in the 1900 election. Although Bryan had opposed acquisition of the Philippines, his economic agenda enjoyed scant support in the Northeast. After McKinley's victory in 1900, the organization continued its efforts exposing the excesses of the occupation â€” chiefly the use of water torture against captured Filipino prisoners â€” but it did not survive to challenge the imperialist ventures of Theodore Roosevelt or Woodrow Wilson during the Progressive Era.26

The Insular Cases: Empire and the Constitution

As opponents of acquiring the Philippines warned, imperialism and the Constitution's promises of equal protection and due process could not be easily reconciled. In a series of decisions collectively known as the Insular Cases, the Supreme Court held that since the new "possessions are inhabited by alien races, differing from us in religion, customs, laws, methods of taxation, and modes of thought, the administration of government and justice, according to Anglo-Saxon principles, may for a time be impossible."27 Three years later, addressing the question of constitutional rights for residents of the colonies, the Court ruled that if the United States should acquire territory "peopled by savages," it was not required to extend full constitutional protections.28 The era's "great dissenter," John Marshall Harlan, opposed the Insular Cases from the same perspective as he dissented from Plessy v. The Decline of Right-Wing Anti-Imperialism

The last vestiges of the Gilded Age pattern came during the administration of Theodore Roosevelt, as Southern Democrats resisted the president's efforts to expand U.S. influence in the Caribbean Basin. Roosevelt's most ambitious policy in this regard started in 1905, when the United States â€” fearful of European intentions after a Hague Court decision regarding Venezuela appeared to justify military action on behalf of bondholders â€” attempted to secure a customs receivership over the Dominican Republic. Since the proposal was presented in the form of a treaty, it required Senate approval and some Democratic support. Georgia Senator Augustus Bacon, joined by Maryland Democrat Isidor Rayner, resisted. Rayner criticized Roosevelt's policy as unfairly benefiting financial interests and worried about the administration transforming the Monroe Doctrine into "an instrument of terror and oppression."30 After two years of frustration, Roosevelt changed tactics and implemented the customs arrangement through an executive agreement â€” so as, he remarked during the debate, to sidestep the "average yahoo among the Democratic senators" who had blindly backed Bacon and Rayner.31

While Bacon, Rayner, and their party allies prevented a customs receivership treaty, Democratic senators showed less willingness to resist the wishes of a Democratic president. Woodrow Wilson's election in 1912, followed by the new president's enunciation of a bold international vision, broke the power of right-wing anti-imperialism in the South. Over the course of his presidency, Wilson enjoyed near-monolithic support from Senate Democrats, with only a small number â€” most prominently Mississippi's James Kimble Vardaman and Missouri's James Reed â€” questioning the president's handling of international affairs. Few, if any, presidents featured as wide a gap between rhetoric and action as Wilson on questions of imperialism. As historian Thomas Knock has most persuasively argued, Wilson sought to integrate domestic reform principles into his international vision â€” but the results would expose the limits of that aspiration.32

Over 6,000 African American soldiers served in the Philippines. Many enlisted hoping military service would prove their patriotism and entitle them to full citizenship rights. The war that began seeking to annihilate Filipino independence aspirations had an ironic outcome: it created the very nation it aimed to suppress. Part VII: Psychology of Imperialism â€” Beyond Pro/Con

Understanding why imperialism happens â€” and why it's so hard to stop â€” requires more than counting votes or measuring military strength. Three scholarly frameworks help explain the deeper logic.

Framework 1: The Meddler's Trap (Cognitive Psychology)

Source: Aroop Mukharji, "The Meddler's Trap," International Security 48:2 (Fall 2023)

Mukharji's concept of the "meddler's trap" describes a psychological mechanism: military intervention creates a new problem that the intervener feels compelled to solve, and the intervener values solving this problem more precisely because their own intervention created it. The tautology: the problem, the solution, and the value of solving it are all consequences of the intervention itself.

Three Interacting Beliefs

  • (1) If we leave, chaos results.
  • (2) Our presence can prevent that chaos.
  • (3) This matters to national interests precisely because we are already there.

The trap: these three beliefs form a closed loop. The intervening power cannot escape without seeming to fail.

This operates through the "endowment effect" â€” a cognitive bias where people overvalue goods they feel they already own or control. Military intervention in the Philippines created an expanded sense of ownership and responsibility. McKinley and subsequent policymakers felt the Philippines were already "ours" (purchased from Spain, occupied militarily) and therefore worth protecting, governing, and integrating at any cost.

Modern Parallels

Vietnam, Iraq, Afghanistan â€” the same meddler's trap recurs. Intervention creates instability; instability justifies continued intervention; continued intervention deepens ownership and commitment; exit becomes politically and psychologically impossible.

Framework 2: The Indian Policy Precedent (Historical Continuity)

Philippine annexation did not emerge spontaneously in 1898. Framework 3: "Will It Pay?" (Economic and Moral Costs)

Source: William Jennings Bryan, "Will It Pay?" New York Journal, January 15, 1899

Bryan reframed the empire question from abstract destiny to concrete costs. His challenge: "If war is to be waged for trade, how much trade ought to be demanded in exchange for a human life?"

The cost-benefit analysis: Direct costs included a standing army of 100,000 troops, "vacant chairs" â€” families without breadwinners. Indirect costs included distraction from labor rights, trust-busting, and monopoly reform. Empire would empower monopolies and weaken reformers' ability to address domestic inequality. Bryan argued that the politics of patriotism and expansion would silence critics and weaken unions.

On claimed benefits: "Commerce is a matter of cost and not a matter of bunting" â€” trade can happen through treaties, commerce, diplomacy. It doesn't require conquest and permanent military occupation.

Discussion: Was Bryan right about the actual costs? How does his domestic distraction argument connect to Gompers's labor critique? Is it possible to put a moral "price" on human life? Can such calculations persuade policy?
